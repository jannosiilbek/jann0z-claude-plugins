# Positioning — ShiftLoop

> The sell foundation. How this product is positioned, differentiated, and talked about.
> Owns the *message*; references product facts (brief.md / product.md / personas.md) by anchor.
> Every asset in `spec/copy/` reads this first and stays faithful to it.

## Positioning statement

> For the **Café Owner** (personas.md) who needs to keep labor cost within budget without leaving shifts
> uncovered (brief `## Opportunity (the need)`), ShiftLoop is a **shift-scheduling app for hourly teams**
> that shows labor cost against budget *while the schedule is being built*. Unlike spreadsheets and group
> texts (brief `## Current alternative`), ShiftLoop catches overspend before the schedule is published —
> not after payroll.

## Category

Shift scheduling for hourly teams.

## Messaging pillars

1. **See the cost before you publish** — the live labor-vs-budget bar updates as shifts are assigned
   (scope F2: budget-aware scheduling).
2. **No more lost swap requests** — swaps run in-app with one-tap approval, not by text (scope F4).
3. **Set it once, repeat it weekly** — copy last week's schedule and adjust (scope F3).

## Stakeholder value (optional — B2B committees only; omit entirely for single-actor / self-serve)

| Committee member (personas `Buyer role`) | What they care about | Pillar/outcome to lead with for them |
|------------------------------------------|----------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Group Operator (Economic-Buyer) | group-wide labor overspend, without micromanaging each café | "See the cost before you publish" — across every Location |
| Café Owner (Champion) | not getting blindsided by a labor blowout on their own café | "See the cost before you publish" — on the build screen |
| Shift Manager (User) | publishing a covered schedule fast, without lost swaps | "No more lost swap requests" |

## Differentiation

- **vs doing nothing / spreadsheets + group texts:** cost is visible *during* scheduling and swap requests
  can't get buried in a text thread — both are in one place with an audit trail.
- **vs an adjacent tool (a generic calendar / Google Sheets template):** ShiftLoop knows the labor budget
  and warns as you assign; a calendar or sheet can't compute labor-% against budget live.
- **vs a direct competitor (When I Work / Homebase):** budget-first, not feature-sprawl — the owner sees
  the one number that matters (labor % of the weekly budget) on the build screen, not buried in a report.

## Objections & responses

| Objection (what the buyer says) | Response (grounded in a real capability or proof) |
|---------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| "I already use a free spreadsheet." | The spreadsheet doesn't tell you you're over budget until payroll; ShiftLoop shows it as you assign. |
| "My staff won't download another app." | Staff get a text/web link to view shifts and request swaps — no install required for employees. |
| "Switching schedules mid-week is risky." | Import this week's spreadsheet in one step; run both for a week before you commit. |

## Anti-personas — who this is NOT for

- Single-location salons or offices with **salaried, fixed schedules** — there's no weekly labor-budget
  tension to manage, so the core value doesn't land.
- Enterprises needing payroll/POS integrations — out of scope (product.md), don't target them.

## Switching forces (JTBD four forces)

- **Push:** the owner discovers labor overspend only after payroll, when it's too late to fix.
- **Pull:** a single live number — labor cost vs budget — on the screen where the schedule is built.
- **Habit:** the spreadsheet is free, familiar, and "works"; rebuilding it feels like wasted effort.
- **Anxiety:** fear that a new tool means retraining staff — answered by the no-install employee link.

## Customer language

- **Words to use:** "over budget", "labor cost", "cover the shift", "swap", "before payroll", "this
  week's schedule".
- **Words to avoid:** "workforce management", "human capital", "scheduling solution", "optimize"
  (insider/vendor framing); "Location" stays capitalized to match glossary spelling.

## Brand voice

- **Tone:** plain-spoken and direct — talks like a café operator who's done payroll, not a software vendor.
- **Personality (3 adjectives):** practical, calm, on-your-side.
- **Style rules:** second person; short sentences; numbers over adjectives; no exclamation marks.

## Proof points

- **Metrics:** `OPEN: measure average labor-% reduction across the first 20 pilot cafés`.
- **Named customers:** `OPEN: get written permission from 2 pilot cafés to name them`.
- **Testimonials:** `OPEN: collect one quote from a pilot owner about catching overspend pre-publish`.
- **Trial (reference, don't restate):** the trial from product.md `## Pricing`, used as risk reversal.
- **Guarantee / risk reversal (positioning owns this):** 30-day money-back, no questions.

## Before → After

- **Before:** the owner builds next week's schedule in a spreadsheet and finds out it blew the labor
  budget only when payroll runs.
- **After:** the owner watches the labor-vs-budget bar as they assign shifts and publishes a covered
  schedule that's already within budget.
