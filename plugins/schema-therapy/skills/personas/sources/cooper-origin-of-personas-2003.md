## Captured account

Cooper recounts how he invented personas as "a practical interaction design tool" while
working (in the mid-1990s, on the Sagent project) on software whose user variation was
dramatic. Rather than designing from his own point of view, he had earlier role-played /
improvised users; on this project he had to *describe* the user models to the team instead.

From user interviews he found participants fell into three distinct groups, differentiated
by **goals, tasks, and skill levels**. He named them — the **first true, goal-directed
personas**:

- **Chuck** — an analyst who used ready-built templates and reports.
- **Cynthia** — an analyst who used the ready-built templates *and* wrote her own, which she
  handed to Chuck to use.
- **Rob** — the IT manager who supported both, could optimize Cynthia's templates, but would
  never originate or use them.

Cooper then presented his designs "from the points of view of Chuck, Cynthia, and Rob
instead of from [his] own." The effect was decisive: "the programmers could clearly see the
sense in my designs because they could identify with these hypothetical archetypes." The team
soon began reasoning *through* the personas — debating "what Cynthia would do" or whether
"Chuck could understand" a given interface element.

### Why it matters for this skill
- Personas are **goal-differentiated**, not demographic-differentiated: the three were split
  by what they were trying to accomplish and at what skill level, not by age/region.
- A persona's payoff is **design adjudication** — it ends "the programmer can always imagine
  he is the user" by precisely defining who the user is.
- The persona names become the team's **shared decision language** — the same downstream
  property the schema-therapy pipeline relies on (08 task models / 09 UI-flows reason per
  persona).
