## Captured content

NN/g divides personas into three types by the research investment behind them. The types
trade rigor against cost; they are not competing theories.

### 1. Proto-personas (lightweight)
- **Definition:** "Lightweight form of ad-hoc personas created with no new research" that
  catalog the team's existing knowledge / best guesses about users.
- **How built:** A 2–4 hour workshop. Participants individually sketch 2–5 simple personas,
  then collectively discuss, combine, and refine into 3–6 final personas.
- **Pros:** No research investment (good for lean / low-UX-maturity teams); make implicit
  assumptions explicit; create shared alignment; can act as hypotheses to validate later.
- **Cons:** Often inaccurate without research; risk reinforcing wrong assumptions; can sour
  perception of personas if seen as valueless.
- **Best for:** Very small orgs / minimal UX maturity that would otherwise skip personas.

### 2. Qualitative personas (RECOMMENDED DEFAULT)
- **Definition:** Personas "based on small-sample qualitative research, such as interviews,
  usability tests, or field studies."
- **How built:** 5–30 user interviews → transcribe → code into themes → find patterns of
  overlap → cluster users sharing core attitudes, goals, and pain points.
- **Pros:** "Solid data-based understanding" at reasonable cost; reveal motivations/context
  analytics can't; balance effort vs. value well.
- **Cons:** Can't say what % of the user base each persona represents; small samples may omit
  rare user types or overrepresent outliers; may draw rigor pushback.
- **Best for:** Most teams seeking efficient, research-grounded personas.

### 3. Statistical personas
- **Definition:** Personas emerging from "statistical analysis to find clusters of similar
  responses" after surveying a large sample.
- **How built:** Start with qualitative research → write survey questions from the themes →
  survey 100+ (ideally 500+) → cluster (latent class analysis, K-means).
- **Pros:** Confidence outliers aren't overrepresented; exact % representation per persona;
  survey can be reused to recruit matching participants.
- **Cons:** Time-intensive and costly; needs statistical expertise; often yields personas
  similar to the purely qualitative ones (wasted effort); clusters may lack design value.
- **Best for:** Large, well-resourced orgs with statistical expertise; "generally not
  recommended."

### Recommendation
Qualitative personas are "the best approach" for most teams. NN/g likens statistical personas
to "cracking a walnut using a hydraulic press."
